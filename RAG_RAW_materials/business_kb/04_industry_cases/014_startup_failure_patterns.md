# Startup Failure Patterns

**Category**: industry_case
**Source**: CB Insights, Y Combinator Library
**Use Case**: Research Agent uses these patterns to flag risks in proposed product directions.

---

## 1. Overview

Most startups fail. The base rate is widely reported in the range of 70–90% over a decade, depending on definition and sample, and the failure rate alone is less interesting than the question of *why*. CB Insights has assembled one of the most cited bodies of evidence on startup failure by analyzing founder post-mortems—self-published essays in which entrepreneurs explain what went wrong after their companies shut down. Aggregating these post-mortems produces a recurring set of failure patterns that hold across categories, geographies, and funding stages.

Several things are worth noting about this evidence base before drawing conclusions from it. The data is self-reported, which introduces a degree of selection and self-attribution bias: founders are more likely to publish post-mortems for narratively coherent failures than for slow, ambiguous ones, and they tend to externalize blame in ways that any retrospective report would. Failure reasons co-occur and reinforce each other—a startup that runs out of cash usually does so because some upstream cause (no market need, weak unit economics, poor execution) rendered fundraising untenable. The percentages reported below are **approximate** and drawn from CB Insights' published analyses, which have been refreshed in multiple iterations over the years.

With those caveats, the patterns are useful. A small number of failure modes account for the great majority of post-mortems, the same modes recur across high-profile public failures, and the lessons that emerge are actionable for founders, investors, and product teams evaluating new directions. This document presents the top reasons by frequency, examines four well-documented failure cases (Quibi, Theranos, Juicero, WeWork), identifies the underlying themes, and distills the lessons.

## 2. Top 10 Reasons Startups Fail

The table below summarizes the most frequently cited failure reasons in CB Insights' analysis of founder post-mortems. The percentages are approximate and represent the share of post-mortems that cite each reason; because most failures are caused by multiple interacting factors, the percentages do not sum to 100%.

| Rank | Reason | Approximate % | Brief explanation |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Ran out of cash / failed to raise new capital | ~38% | The proximate cause of most failures: the runway ended before the company reached the next milestone or fundraise. Often a downstream effect of weaknesses elsewhere. |
| 2 | No market need | ~35% | The product solved a problem that did not exist with sufficient urgency, or the addressable customer count was too small to support a viable business. |
| 3 | Got outcompeted | ~20% | Competitors—established or new—captured the market the startup had hoped to serve, often through better positioning, distribution, or pricing. |
| 4 | Flawed business model | ~19% | Unit economics, pricing, or customer acquisition costs that could not produce sustainable margins regardless of scale. |
| 5 | Regulatory / legal challenges | ~18% | Regulation that the company did not anticipate or could not navigate (data privacy, financial services, healthcare, transportation, gig-economy classification). |
| 6 | Pricing / cost issues | ~15% | Prices set too low to cover costs, too high for the value perceived, or input costs that outpaced revenue. |
| 7 | Not the right team | ~14% | Mismatched skills, lack of domain expertise, weak execution, or absence of co-founder complementarity. |
| 8 | Product mistimed | ~10% | The right product, but too early (market not ready) or too late (incumbents have entrenched). |
| 9 | Poor product | ~8% | The product itself failed users on quality, usability, or reliability, regardless of market demand. |
| 10 | Disharmony among team / investors | ~7% | Founder conflicts, board disputes, or investor disagreements that consumed organizational energy and led to dissolution. |

The top two reasons—running out of cash and no market need—account for a disproportionate share of failures and are deeply linked. A company with strong market need usually finds a way to raise capital; a company with no market need usually cannot, regardless of how much it spends trying.

## 3. Notable Failure Case Studies

Four widely studied failures illustrate how these patterns play out at scale.

**Quibi**
Quibi was a short-form mobile video platform that launched in 2020 with roughly $1.75 billion in funding and an experienced leadership team led by Jeffrey Katzenberg and Meg Whitman. The product offered professionally produced shows in 5–10 minute episodes, designed specifically for vertical viewing on mobile devices. The company shut down within roughly nine months of launch.

Multiple failure modes converged. The product was **mistimed**, launching at the start of the COVID-19 pandemic when consumers were sitting at home rather than commuting (the originally intended viewing context for short-form mobile video). It was **outcompeted** before it began, because TikTok, YouTube, and Instagram already provided short-form mobile video in formats users were habituated to. The **business model** was flawed: Quibi tried to charge subscription fees for content that competing platforms offered free. The product itself faced **product mistakes**, including the inability to view content on a TV and difficulty sharing clips on social media. Quibi is the rare case where significant capital, an experienced team, and a coherent thesis still failed because the underlying market did not exist in the form the company had imagined.

**Theranos**
Theranos was a medical-technology company founded in 2003 that claimed to have developed proprietary technology for performing a wide range of blood tests using a single drop of blood. The company raised substantial venture capital, reached a peak private valuation of approximately $9 billion, and partnered with major retailers to deploy testing centers. Investigative reporting beginning in 2015 revealed that the technology did not work as claimed; the company was found to have used standard commercially available analyzers for most tests while presenting results as the output of its proprietary devices. Founder Elizabeth Holmes was subsequently convicted of fraud.

Theranos is not a typical startup failure—it is a fraud case—and it does not fit the standard CB Insights taxonomy. It is included here because it illustrates a related pattern: in regulated technical domains where claims are difficult for non-specialist investors and journalists to verify, governance failures can persist far longer than in domains where the product either works or does not in obvious ways. The lesson is less about strategy than about the limits of due diligence in deeply technical regulated markets.

**Juicero**
Juicero, founded in 2013, produced a Wi-Fi-connected juicer that pressed proprietary single-serving juice packs. The company raised approximately $120 million in venture capital and sold the device for an initial price near $700 (later reduced). In 2017, *Bloomberg* journalists demonstrated that the juice packs could be squeezed by hand to produce essentially the same result as the machine produced. The company shut down later that year.

Juicero is a textbook case of **flawed business model** combined with **no market need**. The product solved a problem—pressing juice—for which a $700 connected device was an absurd over-engineered answer when the underlying packs worked manually. The unit economics required customers to remain locked into the proprietary pack subscription, and once the manual-squeeze demonstration broke the perceived value of the hardware, the entire model collapsed. Juicero's failure became a widely cited shorthand for over-engineered solutions to non-problems and for venture capital's occasional willingness to fund products whose value proposition does not survive a single round of consumer skepticism.

**WeWork**
WeWork operated a global network of co-working spaces, growing rapidly through aggressive lease commitments and aggressive valuation marks. In 2019, the company filed to IPO at a target valuation that would have approached $47 billion. Public-market investors examined the financial filings, scrutinized the business model and governance, and rejected the offering. The IPO was withdrawn, the valuation collapsed, founder-CEO Adam Neumann departed, and the company subsequently went through restructuring including a much-reduced public listing and bankruptcy proceedings.

WeWork's failure mode was **flawed business model** at scale. The company took on long-term lease liabilities while selling short-term memberships, a structural mismatch that left it deeply exposed to economic downturns. The high valuation depended on positioning the company as a technology platform rather than a real-estate operator, a framing that did not survive financial scrutiny. **Disharmony among team and investors** also played a role; governance concerns surfaced during the IPO process accelerated the collapse. WeWork illustrates how a business model that looks attractive during a long bull market can unwind quickly when the model is exposed to public-market discipline.

## 4. Patterns Across Failures

Several themes recur across the post-mortem data and the case studies.

**Cash is the proximate cause; market is the root cause.** Roughly 38% of post-mortems cite running out of cash, but cash exhaustion is rarely an independent failure—it is the symptom of an upstream problem. Companies with strong product-market fit can almost always raise capital; companies without it cannot raise indefinitely no matter how disciplined their burn. When founders attribute failure to "fundraising," it is usually worth asking what underlying market or product weakness prevented the next round.

**Product-market fit cannot be skipped or assumed.** "No market need" is the second most cited reason and is implicated in nearly every famous failure. Quibi assumed a market for short-form mobile video that already existed in free form; Juicero assumed a market for connected juicing that did not survive a hand-squeeze test; many less famous failures share the same shape. Achieving PMF before scaling is the single most consistently emphasized lesson in the literature.

**Timing is a real and underrated risk.** Approximately 10% of failures are attributed to timing. The right product launched too early dies before the market arrives; the right product launched too late dies because incumbents have entrenched. Timing is harder to assess than market need because it requires forecasting adoption curves rather than measuring current demand.

**Team composition matters as much as product.** Roughly 14% of failures cite team issues, and 7% cite team-investor disharmony. Founder-market fit—whether the founders have the domain expertise, customer empathy, and execution capacity for the specific market they are entering—is sometimes treated as secondary to product-market fit. The data argues it is comparably important.

**Unit economics cannot be deferred indefinitely.** Roughly 19% of failures cite flawed business models and 15% cite pricing or cost issues. Companies that grow without underlying unit economics eventually run into a fundraising round where investors require a path to profitability. WeWork illustrates the public-market version of this discipline; many smaller failures illustrate the venture version.

**Capital can mask weakness for years.** Quibi raised $1.75 billion. Theranos reached a $9 billion valuation. WeWork pursued a $47 billion IPO. Capital does not solve underlying problems; it postpones them, and sometimes magnifies them by allowing the company to scale a flawed model further before the failure becomes terminal.

## 5. Lessons for Builders

**Achieve product-market fit before scaling.** Scaling a product that has not achieved PMF accelerates the failure rather than preventing it. Marketing dollars cannot manufacture demand that does not exist; engineering hires cannot fix a product that is solving the wrong problem. The discipline of staying small until the product genuinely pulls users is unglamorous and is also one of the most consistent predictors of survival.

**Avoid the "everyone is a customer" delusion.** Markets where the founder cannot name a specific persona with a specific job to be done are usually markets that do not exist. Bottom-up customer counts—as in TAM/SAM/SOM analysis—force the team to be specific about who pays, why, and how often. Vague targeting ("anyone who wants to be productive," "everyone who learns") is a leading indicator of "no market need" failures.

**Watch unit economics from day one.** A unit-economics target—gross margin, payback period, lifetime value to acquisition cost ratio—should be defined and tracked from the earliest revenue. Companies that measure unit economics late tend to discover they are bad late, when the business has already scaled the bad numbers.

**Founders' market fit matters as much as product/market fit.** Domain expertise, customer relationships, and execution capacity in the specific market matter more than generic founder talent. A team that is strong in the abstract but lacks specific market knowledge is a recurring pattern in failed startups.

**Test timing assumptions explicitly.** Before committing to a market, articulate what would have to be true for the timing to be right—adoption thresholds, infrastructure readiness, regulatory developments—and test those assumptions. Mistimed products usually fail because the team did not separate "this will work eventually" from "this will work now."

**Treat fundraising as a lagging indicator, not a strategy.** Companies do not become viable by raising; they become viable by building products users want and businesses that can support themselves. Fundraising as a substitute for product-market fit is the most common path to the top failure reason in the table.
