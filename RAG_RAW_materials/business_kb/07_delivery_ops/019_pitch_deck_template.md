# Pitch Deck Template — YC / Sequoia Style

**Category**: delivery_ops
**Source**: Y Combinator, Sequoia Capital, Airbnb (public seed deck)
**Use Case**: Delivery Agent uses this template when generating a pitch deck outline as part of the final package.

---

## 1. Overview

A pitch deck is a short, structured presentation that a startup uses to communicate its opportunity to investors. The deck is not a business plan; it is a conversation starter, designed to be read in five to ten minutes and to either earn a follow-up meeting or settle the question of whether one is warranted. The deck genre has converged over the past two decades on a roughly ten-slide structure that Y Combinator, Sequoia Capital, and most other early-stage investors recognize as standard.

The structure exists because investors evaluate every deck against the same questions: What problem? Who has it? What's the solution? Is the market big enough? Does the product work? Are people using it? How does it make money? Who else is doing this? Why is this team going to win? What do you need from me? Each slide answers one of those questions, in roughly that order. Decks that follow the structure benefit from the investor's pattern recognition; decks that ignore it force the investor to reassemble the argument and lose ground in the process.

Three references anchor modern pitch-deck practice. Y Combinator's library compiles guidance from founders who have raised seed rounds. Sequoia's "Writing a Business Plan" essay distills the questions a venture investor expects a pitch to answer. Airbnb's 2009 seed deck, now public, is a widely cited model of how to communicate a startup's thesis with simplicity and narrative discipline. This document presents the canonical ten-slide structure, examines what made the Airbnb deck successful, articulates the style principles that distinguish strong decks from weak ones, identifies common mistakes, and provides a complete ten-slide outline for an educational coding app for children aged 6–12.

## 2. The 10-Slide Structure

The ten slides below cover the questions investors expect a deck to answer. Each slide has a purpose, a recommended content set, and a set of common mistakes to avoid.

**Slide 1 — Cover**
*Purpose*: Identify the company and set the tone.
*Content*: Company name, one-line tagline that captures what you do, founder names, contact information, date.
*Common mistakes*: Tagline is too clever or too vague to convey what the company does. The cover is a missed opportunity if a reader closes the deck still unsure what business they are about to read about.

**Slide 2 — Problem**
*Purpose*: Establish that a real, painful, and prevalent problem exists.
*Content*: A short articulation of the user problem, ideally grounded in a specific story or scenario. Evidence that the problem is widespread (statistics, user research, support data).
*Common mistakes*: Describing the absence of the proposed solution as the problem ("there is no good app for X") rather than describing the underlying user pain. Stacking generic statistics without a concrete user moment.

**Slide 3 — Solution**
*Purpose*: Show that the product addresses the problem in a credible way.
*Content*: A plain-language description of the product. A single sentence the reader can repeat to a colleague after closing the deck.
*Common mistakes*: Burying the solution in jargon. Listing features instead of describing the user outcome. Promising a platform when the reader is still trying to understand the first product.

**Slide 4 — Market Size**
*Purpose*: Demonstrate that the opportunity is large enough to support a venture-scale outcome.
*Content*: TAM, SAM, and SOM figures with bottom-up reasoning, sourced where possible. A clear statement of who the customer is and what they pay annually.
*Common mistakes*: The infamous "if we capture just 1% of a $100B market" framing, which signals to investors that the team has not done the bottom-up work. Treating TAM as a revenue forecast rather than as a ceiling.

**Slide 5 — Product**
*Purpose*: Show the product, not describe it.
*Content*: Screenshots, a short demo video, or a live walk-through. The principle is "show don't tell"—the reader should be able to see the product working in roughly fifteen seconds of attention.
*Common mistakes*: Mockups that look like marketing renders instead of a real working product. Walls of text where screenshots would do better.

**Slide 6 — Traction**
*Purpose*: Prove that the product is working in the market.
*Content*: Specific metrics—users, revenue, retention, growth rate, NPS, customer logos, letters of intent. For early-stage companies, traction is often the single most important slide; investors discount stories aggressively when traction is absent or thin.
*Common mistakes*: Vanity metrics (downloads, total registered users, total impressions) instead of meaningful ones (weekly active users, retention, paying customers, growth rate). Charts without axis labels or time scales. Burying the chart that actually goes up.

**Slide 7 — Business Model**
*Purpose*: Show how the company captures value from the market.
*Content*: Pricing, revenue model (subscription, transaction, advertising), unit economics where available (LTV, CAC, payback). A clear statement of who pays, how much, and how often.
*Common mistakes*: Hand-waving about monetization with phrases like "many monetization paths." Listing five revenue streams when one is reality and four are speculation.

**Slide 8 — Competition**
*Purpose*: Demonstrate that the team understands the landscape and has identified a defensible position.
*Content*: A 2x2 matrix or a feature comparison table. Specific named competitors, including the strongest ones. A clear statement of what the company does that competitors do not, or does meaningfully better.
*Common mistakes*: Claiming "no competition," which to investors signals either a market that does not exist or a team that has not done its homework. Choosing weak competitors to make the comparison favorable. A 2x2 in which every competitor is in the bottom-left and the company alone is in the top-right—investors recognize this as marketing rather than analysis.

**Slide 9 — Team**
*Purpose*: Convince the investor that this team is the right team to build this company.
*Content*: Founders' names, photos, prior roles relevant to the problem domain, and any prior outcomes (companies built, exits, technical achievements, domain expertise). Brief mention of key advisors or hires if material.
*Common mistakes*: Generic resumes that do not connect to the company's market. Founder-market fit is a real evaluation criterion; teams that cannot articulate why they are the right people for this specific problem signal weakness.

**Slide 10 — Ask**
*Purpose*: Tell the investor exactly what is being requested.
*Content*: Funding amount sought, valuation or terms (when appropriate to the conversation stage), and a use-of-funds breakdown that ties the requested amount to specific milestones (e.g., "$2M to reach 10,000 paying subscribers and ship the school-channel product over 18 months").
*Common mistakes*: Omitting the ask entirely, which forces the investor to guess. Vague use of funds ("scale the team and grow"). Asking for an amount that is inconsistent with the stage or the milestones.

## 3. Why Airbnb's Seed Deck Is a Model

The 2009 Airbnb seed deck, used to raise $600,000 and now widely available online, runs to roughly twelve slides. It is held up as a model for several reasons that remain relevant.

The deck is **short**. Each slide says one thing, and the reader can absorb the entire deck in five minutes. The discipline of one-thing-per-slide forces the team to identify what each slide is actually for.

The deck is **story-driven**. The problem slide describes a specific scenario—price-sensitive travelers struggling to find affordable lodging during conferences when hotels are full or expensive—rather than reciting industry statistics. The story makes the problem feel real before the numbers arrive to confirm it.

The deck is **simple in design**. There is little visual flourish. The slides are uncluttered, the typography is plain, and the screenshots are not heavily styled. The lack of polish is not a defect; it is a feature, because it directs attention to the substance.

The deck has a **clear ask** and a **defensible market analysis**. The market sizing is grounded in identifiable segments rather than aspirational top-down arithmetic. The competitive analysis names real competitors, including Craigslist, and articulates how Airbnb is different.

The lessons generalize. Length should be set by what the argument requires, typically ten to fifteen slides. Story should precede statistics. Design should serve clarity, not impress. The ask should be specific. A deck that follows these principles can be written by a small team in a few days; a deck that ignores them can consume weeks of design effort and still fail to communicate the opportunity.

## 4. Style Principles

Three principles distinguish strong decks from weak ones.

**Fewer than thirty words per slide.** Slides are not documents. A slide with two paragraphs of text is asking the audience to read, which they will do instead of listening, and they will read faster than the speaker can present. The rule of thumb is fewer than thirty words per slide; many strong slides have fewer than ten. Headlines should carry the assertion, and the body should support it visually—charts, screenshots, diagrams, a single quote.

**Story over data, but data confirms.** Investors are persuaded by stories that are then validated by data. A problem slide that opens with a specific user moment and is followed by a number ("17 million households like this in the United States alone") is more persuasive than one that opens with the number. The story makes the data meaningful; the data makes the story credible.

**Clarity over polish.** A plain deck with a sharp argument outperforms a beautifully designed deck with a muddled argument. Investors read decks for substance; design that obscures the argument actively works against the team. The standard is to make the deck as polished as it needs to be to not distract, and not more polished than that.

A fourth principle is implicit in the genre: **the deck is for the investor, not for the team**. Founders sometimes use the deck as a vehicle for everything they want to say about the company. A useful editing pass is to read each slide and ask, "Why does the investor care about this?" Slides that fail the test should be cut.

## 5. Common Mistakes

- **Too many words per slide.** The single most common deck failure. Audiences read silently rather than listening to the founder; the founder loses control of the narrative.
- **No traction data.** Early-stage decks are sometimes written before traction exists, but where any data exists, it must be on the slide. Investors discount stories aggressively when traction is absent or hidden.
- **Vague market sizing.** "If we capture 1% of a $100B market" signals to investors that the team has not done the bottom-up work. Replace with specific customer counts and ARPU.
- **Weak team slide.** Generic resumes that do not connect to the market. The team slide should answer "why this team for this problem."
- **Missing or vague ask.** A deck without a clear ask forces the investor to guess; vague asks ("we're raising a seed round") do not anchor the conversation.
- **Hidden competition.** "We have no competition" or unflattering comparisons to weak competitors signal either a missing market or a team that has not done its homework. Name the strongest competitors and explain how this team will win.
- **Overdesigned slides.** Heavy templating, animation, and styling that draw attention away from the argument. The deck is a business document, not a design portfolio.
- **Too long.** Twelve to fifteen slides is the sweet spot for early-stage decks. Twenty-five-slide decks usually contain ten slides of useful content and fifteen of filler; investors skim the rest.

## 6. Example: 10-Slide Outline for Educational Coding App

The following is a complete ten-slide outline for **CodeCub**, a hypothetical Python-based educational coding app for children aged 6–12. Each slide includes a one-line headline and the content that would appear on the slide.

**Slide 1 — Cover**
- *Headline*: CodeCub — real coding for kids 6–12.
- *Content*: Company logo, founder names (Maya Chen and Daniel Park), contact email, date.

**Slide 2 — Problem**
- *Headline*: Parents want their kids to learn to code. The current options don't work.
- *Content*: A short scene: an 8-year-old opens Scratch, drags blocks for thirty minutes, builds a moving cat, and is bored by the next day. A 10-year-old opens a Python tutorial and gives up at "import." Supporting evidence: 38 parent interviews, two-thirds of whom reported the same drop-off pattern.

**Slide 3 — Solution**
- *Headline*: Real Python from lesson one, designed for how kids actually play.
- *Content*: One-sentence description: "CodeCub teaches children 6–12 to write real Python by guiding them through games they build themselves, with a friendly mascot and parent-visible progress evidence." A single screenshot showing a child's first running program.

**Slide 4 — Market Size**
- *Headline*: $1.4B serviceable opportunity in the English-speaking paying segment.
- *Content*: TAM $48B (global connected children 6–12 at $120/year subscription, illustrative). SAM $1.44B (English-comfortable, paying-willing connected families). Year-3 SOM $36M (300,000 paying subscribers at $120/year). Bottom-up logic noted on the slide.

**Slide 5 — Product**
- *Headline*: A child writes Python on day one.
- *Content*: Three screenshots: (1) the child's first lesson with real syntax visible; (2) a small game running that the child built; (3) the parent's weekly progress email. A 30-second demo video link.

**Slide 6 — Traction**
- *Headline*: 3,200 paying subscribers, 62% Day-30 retention, 18% month-over-month growth.
- *Content*: A line chart showing weekly active learners over the past six months. Key metrics: paying subscribers, Day-30 retention, parent CSAT (4.5/5 from in-app surveys), Net Promoter Score (62). Two short parent quotes from real users.

**Slide 7 — Business Model**
- *Headline*: $9.99/month consumer subscription, $4/seat school tier.
- *Content*: Pricing tiers (consumer monthly, consumer annual at 25% discount, school per-seat). Unit economics: blended ARPU $96/year, CAC $32, payback 4 months, gross margin 78%. School channel listed as a complementary motion to be expanded in Year 2.

**Slide 8 — Competition**
- *Headline*: The market splits between free block-based tools and overpriced bootcamp-style products. We're neither.
- *Content*: A 2x2 with axes "real syntax → block-based" and "free → paid." Scratch and Code.org in free/block-based; Tynker in paid/mostly-block-based; expensive tutoring services in paid/real-syntax; CodeCub in paid/real-syntax with parent-visible outcomes. A short paragraph naming the three strongest competitors and articulating CodeCub's specific differentiation.

**Slide 9 — Team**
- *Headline*: Curriculum, product, and a real classroom track record.
- *Content*: Maya Chen, co-founder and CEO: former K–8 computer science teacher, eight years; led the CS curriculum at a 1,200-student charter school. Daniel Park, co-founder and CTO: former senior engineer at a children's media company; built and shipped two consumer apps used by over 500,000 children. Advisor: a learning-sciences researcher with 15 years studying age-appropriate computer science pedagogy.

**Slide 10 — Ask**
- *Headline*: Raising $3M seed to reach 25,000 paying subscribers and launch the school channel.
- *Content*: Funding amount: $3M. Use of funds: $1.5M product and engineering (Chromebook web version, AI tutoring layer); $0.9M growth (paid acquisition, content partnerships); $0.4M school-channel sales (one founding sales hire and pilot programs); $0.2M operations and runway buffer. 18-month runway. Targeted close in the next 90 days.

The outline runs to ten slides, each carrying one assertion supported by visual evidence. A reader who closes the deck after the cover, the problem, and the traction slides should already know whether they want to take a meeting; the remaining seven slides earn the meeting if the first three made the case.
