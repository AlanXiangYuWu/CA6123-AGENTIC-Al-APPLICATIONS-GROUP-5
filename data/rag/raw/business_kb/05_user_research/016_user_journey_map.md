# User Journey Map

**Category**: user_research
**Source**: Nielsen Norman Group
**Use Case**: Product Agent uses this artifact when designing user flows in the PRD.

---

## 1. Overview

A **User Journey Map (UJM)** is a visualization of the process a user goes through to accomplish a specific goal with a product or service. Rather than describing a product as a static set of features, a journey map traces the user's experience as it unfolds over time—from the moment they first encounter a problem, through the steps they take to solve it, to the long-term state of using or leaving the chosen solution. The artifact captures not only what the user does at each step, but also what they are thinking, how they are feeling, where they touch the brand, and where the experience breaks down.

Journey maps serve three purposes that are difficult to achieve with feature-list documents. They build **empathy** by forcing the team to see the experience from the user's perspective rather than from the product's internal architecture. They produce **alignment** across product, design, engineering, marketing, and customer support, all of whom influence pieces of the journey but rarely see the whole. And they expose **gaps**—the moments where the user expects something the product does not deliver, where touchpoints fail to connect, or where emotional friction exceeds the value the product provides.

This document defines the standard components of a journey map, walks through the common stages from awareness through advocacy, contrasts the major visual formats, presents a step-by-step process for creating a map, and provides a complete example for a parent discovering and adopting a kids' coding app.

---

## 2. Standard Components

A complete user journey map contains the following components. Each adds a layer of context; together they produce a multi-dimensional view of the experience.

- **Persona** — The specific user the map describes. Journey maps are persona-specific; a single map for "all users" loses the detail that makes the artifact useful. If multiple personas have meaningfully different journeys, draw multiple maps.
- **Scenario** — What the user is trying to accomplish. The scenario constrains the map's scope (for example, "first-time signup and first lesson" versus "daily return usage") and prevents the map from sprawling into every possible behavior.
- **Stages** — The phases the user moves through over time. The standard stages run from awareness through advocacy and are detailed in Section 3.
- **Actions** — The concrete things the user does at each stage. Actions are observable behaviors, not internal states.
- **Thoughts** — What the user is thinking at each stage. Often expressed as first-person quotes drawn from research.
- **Feelings / Emotions** — The user's emotional state, often plotted as a curve that rises and falls across stages. The emotional curve highlights the moments of friction and delight that determine whether the user continues.
- **Touchpoints** — Where the user interacts with the brand or product: website, app, email, push notification, support chat, social media, third-party reviews, word of mouth.
- **Pain points** — Frustrations, barriers, confusions, or unmet expectations at each stage.
- **Opportunities** — Specific places where the experience could be improved, derived from the pain points.

The persona and scenario are stated once at the top of the map; the remaining components are filled in stage by stage.

## 3. Common Stages

The standard stage sequence covers the full lifecycle from first encounter to long-term advocacy. Not every product or scenario uses all stages; some maps focus tightly on a subset (for example, only awareness through onboarding for a launch).

- **Awareness** — The user first becomes aware that the product or solution category exists. They may not yet feel the need acutely; they are encountering, not searching.
- **Consideration** — The user is actively evaluating options. They compare alternatives, read reviews, ask peers, examine pricing.
- **Decision** — The user commits to a specific product, typically by signing up, downloading, or purchasing.
- **Onboarding** — The user takes the first steps inside the product. This is where many users either form a habit or churn permanently; onboarding is disproportionately consequential for long-term retention.
- **Engagement** — The user uses the product regularly to derive value. The engagement stage is where the bulk of the user lifecycle is spent.
- **Retention** — The user continues to use the product over time, deepens usage, or upgrades. Retention is the result of sustained engagement plus moments that justify continued investment.
- **Advocacy** — The user actively recommends the product to others, shares it on social media, writes reviews, or refers friends. Advocacy is the strongest signal of long-term satisfaction and a major organic growth channel.

The stages are sequential but not strictly linear—users can re-enter earlier stages (a retained user reconsidering during a price increase, an engaged user advocating before reaching long-term retention).

## 4. Why Journey Maps Matter

Three benefits justify the effort required to build and maintain a journey map.

**Empathy.** Reading a journey map forces the team to see what the user actually experiences—the moments of confusion, the wait times, the friction at handoffs between teams. Designers, engineers, and executives who interact with the product through internal admin tools often have a fundamentally different experience from the user; journey maps make that gap visible.

**Alignment.** Most products are built and operated by cross-functional teams: marketing owns awareness, sales owns consideration, product owns onboarding and engagement, support owns the recovery moments, success owns retention. Each function tends to optimize its own stage in isolation. A shared journey map gives the team a single picture in which the contributions of each function are visible alongside the user's overall experience.

**Gap identification.** Pain points and emotional dips on the map point to specific places where the experience breaks. Touchpoint handoffs (marketing email to product onboarding, support resolution to retention) are common gap locations because they cross functional boundaries. The map makes these breaks legible in a way feature backlogs do not.

## 5. Common Formats

Three formats dominate practice; each emphasizes different aspects of the journey.

| Format | Best for | Strength | Trade-off |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Matrix table** | Comprehensive documentation | Captures all components systematically | Less visually striking; can feel dense |
| **Swimlane diagram** | Multi-actor or multi-system flows | Shows handoffs across functions or systems clearly | Less natural for emotional and qualitative content |
| **Curve diagram** (emotional journey) | Communicating emotional highs and lows | Visually compelling for executives and stakeholders | Loses detail; usually paired with a matrix for completeness |

The matrix table is the most common working format and is the format used in the example below. The curve diagram is often produced as a complementary visualization for stakeholder presentations.

## 6. Step-by-step: How to Create a User Journey Map

1. **Choose a persona and a scenario.** State both explicitly at the top of the map. Resist the temptation to map "all users doing everything"; specificity is the source of insight.
2. **Define the stages.** Use the standard stages as a starting point and adapt them to the scenario. A purchase journey may compress engagement; a habit-product journey may emphasize it.
3. **Gather research.** Draw actions, thoughts, and feelings from real evidence: user interviews, support tickets, analytics, session recordings. Maps built from imagination produce imagined insights.
4. **Fill in actions for each stage.** What does the user concretely do? Use observable verbs.
5. **Add thoughts and feelings.** Quote the user's voice where possible. Plot the emotional curve to make the highs and lows visible.
6. **Identify touchpoints.** List every channel and surface the user interacts with at each stage.
7. **Mark pain points.** Wherever the emotional curve dips or the user hesitates, name the friction.
8. **Derive opportunities.** Each pain point should pair with a specific opportunity—a concrete change to the product or experience.
9. **Validate with users.** Walk a few representative users through the map and confirm the actions, feelings, and pain points match their actual experience.
10. **Keep it current.** Treat the map as a living document; update it when the product or the user behavior changes materially.

## 7. Example: Parent Discovers and Adopts a Kids' Coding App

**Persona**: Maya, a 38-year-old parent of an 8-year-old daughter. Technology-comfortable but not a developer. Concerned about screen time but open to educational alternatives.
**Scenario**: Maya discovers, evaluates, signs up for, and adopts a kids' coding app over a four-week period.

| Stage | Actions | Thoughts | Feelings | Touchpoints | Pain points | Opportunities |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Awareness** | Sees a friend's social-media post about her child's coding project; reads a parenting newsletter mentioning kids' coding apps. | "Coding seems like a useful skill. I wonder if my daughter would like it." | Mildly curious; not urgent. | Social media, newsletter, peer conversation. | Awareness is passive; no specific product comes to mind. | Build a presence in trusted parenting newsletters; encourage user-generated social posts of kid-built projects. |
| **Consideration** | Searches "best coding apps for kids 8 year old"; reads three review articles; checks the app store; asks two friends in a parents' group chat. | "There are a lot of these. Some are free. Why would I pay?" | Overwhelmed by options; skeptical of paid tiers. | Search engine, review sites, app store, peer recommendation. | Hard to differentiate paid from free options on first read; reviews focus on features rather than learning outcomes. | Lead with measurable learning outcomes and parent-visible progress evidence; publish a clear comparison against free alternatives. |
| **Decision** | Downloads the app, starts a 7-day free trial, enters the child's age, sets up a parent account. | "Let's see if she likes it. I can cancel if it doesn't stick." | Hopeful but provisional; ready to walk away. | App store, app onboarding, email confirmation. | Long sign-up form before the child can try anything; payment method requested up front feels pushy. | Defer payment-method capture until after the first lesson; let the child try a lesson immediately while parent setup happens in parallel. |
| **Onboarding** | Sits with daughter for the first lesson; daughter completes one short lesson; parent receives a welcome email and first progress note. | "She actually liked it. The first lesson was clearer than I expected." | Pleasantly surprised; engaged. | App, email, on-screen celebration. | First lesson is good, but the next-step path is unclear—parent isn't sure whether to schedule daily practice or let the child explore. | Provide a clear suggested cadence for the first week; offer parent guidance on how to support without taking over. |
| **Engagement** | Daughter completes 3–4 lessons per week; parent reviews weekly progress emails; daughter shows off a small game to grandparents. | "She's actually building things. I want to keep this going." | Proud; reassured that screen time is producing learning. | App, weekly email, family conversation. | Around week three, daughter hits a difficult lesson and gets stuck; the in-app hint gives away too much, undermining her sense of accomplishment. | Redesign hint system to scaffold without solving; add an option for parents to be notified when a child is stuck so they can help in person. |
| **Retention & Advocacy** | Renews subscription after trial; shares daughter's published mini-game in the parents' group chat; mentions the app at a school event. | "This is one of the rare apps I'm glad we pay for." | Confident; mildly evangelistic. | App, social media, peer conversation, school community. | No built-in way to share or refer; sharing is manual. | Add a one-tap share for child-built projects with parental approval; introduce a referral program with a clear value exchange. |

The map exposes specific actionable findings. The decision stage suffers from a payment-first onboarding flow; the onboarding stage lacks cadence guidance; the engagement stage has a hint-system design problem that undermines the child's sense of competence; the advocacy stage is missing built-in sharing infrastructure. Each finding maps to a concrete product change, and the changes can be prioritized by their impact on the corresponding stage.

## 8. Tools and References

Common tools for journey mapping include **Miro** and **Figma** for collaborative diagramming, **Smaply** as a dedicated journey-mapping tool, and basic **paper and sticky notes** for early-stage workshops. The choice of tool matters less than the rigor of the underlying research; a paper map built on real user evidence is more valuable than a polished digital map built on assumption.
