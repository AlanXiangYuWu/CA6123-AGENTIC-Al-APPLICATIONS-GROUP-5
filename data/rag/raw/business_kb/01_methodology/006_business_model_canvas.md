# Business Model Canvas

**Category**: methodology
**Source**: Strategyzer (Alex Osterwalder), Wikipedia
**Use Case**: Product Agent uses this framework to model the full business — including value, channels, and operations — when writing PRDs for products with a clear business model.

---

## 1. Overview

The **Business Model Canvas (BMC)** is a strategic management tool that describes how a business creates, delivers, and captures value—rendered as a single-page visual chart of nine interconnected blocks. Developed by **Alex Osterwalder** in his 2004 PhD thesis at the University of Lausanne and popularized in the 2010 book *Business Model Generation* co-authored with Yves Pigneur, the BMC has become one of the most widely adopted strategic frameworks in modern business practice. According to Strategyzer (the company Osterwalder co-founded), the canvas has been used by more than 5 million people globally across startups, established enterprises, nonprofits, and government agencies.

The BMC's strength lies in its completeness on a single page. A business model that traditionally required a fifty-page document can be expressed as a structured one-page artifact in which the relationships between value creation (what customers receive), value delivery (how they receive it), and value capture (how the company earns from it) are simultaneously visible. This makes the BMC particularly useful for cross-functional alignment, scenario testing, and identifying gaps where one block does not coherently support another.

This document covers the BMC's origin, the content of each of the nine blocks, the structural meaning of its two sides (the value-facing right and the efficiency-facing left), the comparison with the Lean Canvas variant for early-stage startups, a complete filled example for an educational coding app, and the common pitfalls that diminish the canvas's value when it is filled in carelessly.

## 2. Origin and Purpose

Alex Osterwalder developed the Business Model Canvas as part of his **2004 PhD thesis at the University of Lausanne**, supervised by Yves Pigneur. The thesis advanced the argument that business models could be represented as a structured ontology rather than as freeform narrative—a set of interrelated components that any business has, regardless of industry. The nine-block format crystallized this ontology into a tool that practitioners could use directly, without academic specialization.

The canvas reached widespread adoption through the **2010 book *Business Model Generation***, which Osterwalder and Pigneur co-authored along with contributions from a global community of practitioners. The book itself was structurally innovative—visual, modular, and produced through a crowdfunded contributor model—and it positioned the BMC as a practical instrument rather than a theoretical artifact. Subsequent work through Osterwalder's company **Strategyzer** has built training, software, and complementary canvases (notably the Value Proposition Canvas) on the same foundation.

The BMC's purpose is to make a business model **explicit, shared, and testable**. Explicit, because writing it down forces the team to articulate assumptions that often remain tacit. Shared, because a single-page artifact creates common ground across product, sales, finance, and operations. Testable, because each block is a hypothesis that can be falsified by evidence; a canvas that survives contact with real customers and real operations is stronger than one that does not.

## 3. The Nine Blocks

| # | Block | What to write | Example for an educational coding app |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Customer Segments** | The distinct groups of people or organizations the business aims to serve. List each segment separately if their needs, behaviors, or willingness to pay differ. | Primary: technology-comfortable parents of children aged 6–12 in English-speaking markets. Secondary: K-12 schools with computer-science-education mandates. Tertiary: after-school programs and coding-club operators. |
| 2 | **Value Propositions** | The bundle of products and services that creates value for each customer segment. State the value, not the feature list. | For parents: a credible, screen-time-worthy way to teach their child real coding skills, with weekly evidence of learning. For schools: a COPPA- and FERPA-compliant Python curriculum that meets state standards with minimal teacher prep. |
| 3 | **Channels** | The means by which the company reaches and delivers value to its segments: communication, distribution, and sales channels. | App Store and Google Play for direct distribution; parent-focused YouTube creators and parenting newsletters for awareness; school-district pilots through state CS-education nonprofits; an in-app referral program for organic growth. |
| 4 | **Customer Relationships** | The type of relationship the company establishes with each segment: personal assistance, self-service, automated, community, co-creation, etc. | Parents: largely self-service through the app, with automated weekly progress emails and chat-based support. Schools: dedicated account management with onboarding workshops and quarterly check-ins. Community: a moderated parent forum and a kid-safe creation gallery. |
| 5 | **Revenue Streams** | How the business earns money from each segment, including pricing model and pricing tactics. | Consumer subscriptions: $9.99/month or $89/year. School-channel licensing: $4 per student per year, billed annually. Gift subscriptions during seasonal peaks. No advertising in any tier (a deliberate choice for the COPPA-sensitive audience). |
| 6 | **Key Resources** | The most important assets required to make the business model work: physical, intellectual, human, financial. | Intellectual: proprietary curriculum, age-banded lesson library, brand. Human: curriculum designers (former K–8 CS teachers), engineering team, school-channel sales staff. Financial: $3M seed round providing 18 months of runway. Technical: cloud infrastructure, content delivery network. |
| 7 | **Key Activities** | The most important things the company must do well to deliver its value proposition. | Continuous curriculum development and refresh; product engineering on iOS, Android, and web; school-district sales and customer success; learning-outcome research; content moderation in user-facing creation features. |
| 8 | **Key Partnerships** | The network of suppliers and partners that make the business model work. | Cloud and infrastructure providers (AWS or GCP); App Store and Google Play as platform partners; state-level CS-education nonprofits as school-channel partners; parent-influencer creators as awareness partners; learning-sciences researchers as advisory partners. |
| 9 | **Cost Structure** | The costs incurred from operating the model: fixed and variable, with the largest line items called out. | Fixed: engineering and curriculum salaries (the largest line), cloud infrastructure, office and operations. Variable: paid acquisition (target CAC ~$32), App Store and Google Play revenue share, payment processing, content production for periodic curriculum refreshes, customer support. |

A useful discipline when filling each block: state the content specifically and link it to other blocks. A customer segment that is not served by any listed value proposition signals a gap; a channel that does not reach any listed segment signals an inconsistency; a key activity that does not produce a listed value proposition or revenue stream signals operational waste.

## 4. Two Sides of the Canvas

The nine blocks are arranged around a central spine (Value Propositions) with two distinct halves that have structural meaning.

**The right side — value-facing (external).**
Customer Segments, Value Propositions, Channels, Customer Relationships, and Revenue Streams sit on the right side of the canvas. These blocks describe how the business engages with the outside world: who it serves, what it offers them, how it reaches them, what kind of relationship it forms, and how that relationship produces revenue. The right side answers the question, "What does this business do for its customers, and what does it earn from doing it?"

**The left side — efficiency-facing (internal).**
Key Resources, Key Activities, Key Partnerships, and Cost Structure sit on the left side. These blocks describe how the business operates internally to deliver the value promised on the right side: what it owns, what it does, who it depends on, and what those operations cost. The left side answers the question, "What does this business need to do, have, and pay for, to deliver its value proposition profitably?"

The structural separation is significant. Every left-side block exists to support the right side; the value of an internal capability is not intrinsic but derived from the customer-facing value it enables. A cleanly designed BMC shows direct lines from each left-side block to the right-side outcomes it supports. A disorganized BMC shows left-side activities and resources that do not connect to any right-side value—an indicator of operational drift.

The two sides also tend to attract different functions in cross-functional reviews. Marketing, sales, and product teams gravitate toward the right side; operations, engineering, and finance teams gravitate toward the left. Reviewing the full canvas together exposes whether the two sides are consistent with each other.

## 5. BMC vs Lean Canvas

The **Lean Canvas**, created by Ash Maurya in 2010, is a startup-focused variant of the BMC. Both share the same nine-block, single-page format, but the Lean Canvas replaces four blocks to address the dominant risks of early-stage ventures.

| Block (BMC) | Block (Lean Canvas) | Why the change |
|---|---|---|
| Key Activities | Solution | Early-stage startups need to articulate what they are building, not how they will operate it at scale. |
| Key Resources | Key Metrics | Startups need to know what to measure to learn whether the model is working, not what assets to manage. |
| Key Partnerships | Unfair Advantage | Partnership networks are premature for unvalidated businesses; the harder question is what defends the company once it exists. |
| Customer Relationships | Problem | The most foundational risk for a startup is whether it is solving a real problem; relationship design comes later. |

The two canvases serve different stages of a company's life. The **Lean Canvas** is best suited to early-stage startups whose central risks are problem-solution fit and customer-segment validity. The **BMC** is best suited to **established or scaling businesses** whose problem-solution fit is validated and whose central questions are operational: how to scale delivery, manage partnerships, and structure costs efficiently.

The choice is not zero-sum. A startup may use the Lean Canvas during discovery and switch to the BMC once the business model has matured. Many teams maintain both at different points in the company's life, with the Lean Canvas evolving into the BMC as operational questions begin to dominate strategic ones.

A complementary tool worth noting: the **Value Proposition Canvas**, also from Strategyzer, zooms into the Value Proposition and Customer Segments blocks of the BMC and unpacks them into finer detail (customer **jobs**, **pains**, and **gains**, matched against product **gain creators**, **pain relievers**, and **products and services**). The Value Proposition Canvas is used in conjunction with the BMC, not as a replacement.

## 6. Example: BMC for Educational Coding App for Kids

The following is a complete filled Business Model Canvas for **CodeCub**, a Python-based educational coding app for children aged 6–12, assumed to be at a stage where consumer product-market fit is established and the company is scaling toward the school channel.

**1. Customer Segments**
Primary: technology-comfortable parents of children aged 6–12 in English-speaking markets, household income above the median. Secondary: K-12 schools (initially elementary and middle schools) in US states with computer-science-education mandates. Tertiary: after-school program operators and coding-club providers serving the same age group.

**2. Value Propositions**
For parents: a credible, screen-time-worthy way to teach their child real Python with weekly visible evidence of learning, designed to bridge the well-known gap from block-based tools (Scratch, Code.org) into transferable programming skills. For schools: a COPPA- and FERPA-compliant curriculum aligned to state CS standards, with teacher-friendly assignment tools and minimal prep time. For after-school programs: a turnkey coding curriculum that can be delivered with non-specialist staff.

**3. Channels**
Awareness: parent-focused YouTube creators (mid-tier rather than top-tier), parenting newsletters and active subreddits, partnerships with state-level CS-education nonprofits whose newsletters reach district administrators. Distribution: App Store and Google Play for consumer; direct sales for school accounts; a Chromebook-compatible web version for classroom use. Sales: self-service consumer signup through a 7-day trial; relationship-based sales for school accounts.

**4. Customer Relationships**
Parents: predominantly self-service inside the app, supplemented by automated weekly progress emails, in-app chat support, and a moderated parent community forum. Schools: dedicated account management with onboarding workshops, quarterly check-ins with school administrators, and a teacher community for curriculum sharing. Children: protected, kid-safe creation features (game remixing, project sharing within parent-approved bounds), with strict moderation and no direct messaging.

**5. Revenue Streams**
Consumer subscriptions: $9.99 per month or $89 per year (a 25% annual discount). School licensing: $4 per student per year, sold via annual contracts to districts and individual schools. Gift subscriptions during seasonal peaks (back-to-school, holidays). Deliberately no advertising in any tier; the audience demographics make ads operationally and reputationally costly. Free 7-day trial for consumer signups; free pilot semester for qualifying school customers.

**6. Key Resources**
Intellectual: the proprietary age-banded curriculum, the lesson library, the brand and parent-trust position, accumulated learning-outcomes data from the active user base. Human: curriculum designers (the founding team includes three former K–8 CS teachers), engineers, school-channel salespeople, customer-success staff, content moderators. Financial: capital reserves from the most recent Series A providing approximately 24 months of runway at current burn. Technical: the multi-platform application infrastructure and the content-delivery pipeline.

**7. Key Activities**
Continuous curriculum development—new lesson sequences and ongoing refinement of existing ones based on completion and learning-outcome data. Product engineering across iOS, Android, and the new web platform. School-channel sales and customer success: outbound to districts in mandate states, inbound from inquiries, ongoing support to active schools. Learning-outcomes research: instrumented assessment of skill acquisition to support marketing claims and curriculum refinement. Content moderation: review of user-generated creations in the kid-safe sharing gallery.

**8. Key Partnerships**
Cloud and infrastructure: AWS as the primary cloud provider. Platform: Apple's App Store and Google Play as distribution and billing partners (with the corresponding revenue share). School channel: state-level CS-education nonprofits (e.g., a state's STEM education foundation) as awareness and credibility partners; one or two regional educational distributors for district procurement. Awareness: a roster of 8–12 mid-tier parent-influencer YouTube creators on rolling content partnerships. Advisory: two learning-sciences researchers and one child-safety policy advisor.

**9. Cost Structure**
Fixed costs (largest line items first): engineering salaries; curriculum and content team salaries; school-channel sales and customer-success staff; cloud infrastructure (modest because lessons are cached client-side); office and operations. Variable costs: paid acquisition (current blended CAC approximately $32, with a payback of approximately 4 months); App Store and Google Play revenue share on consumer transactions; payment processing; ongoing content production (illustration, voiceover, periodic curriculum refreshes); customer support staffing scaled to active subscriber count.

This canvas is intended to evolve. As the school channel scales, Key Partnerships will likely expand to include additional educational distributors and possibly state-level procurement intermediaries; Cost Structure will tilt further toward sales and customer success; the Customer Relationships block may differentiate further between consumer and institutional motions. Treating the BMC as a living artifact, revised quarterly, is what keeps it useful.

## 7. Common Pitfalls

- **Treating the canvas as a static document instead of a hypothesis to test.** A BMC filled once and filed away becomes wallpaper. Every block contains assumptions that should be tested against evidence and revised on a defined cadence (typically quarterly, or after material market changes).
- **Overpacking single blocks with too many items.** Listing fifteen channels or a dozen customer segments dilutes the analytical signal. Force the list to the most important items; if a block genuinely needs many entries, segment the canvas (one canvas per business unit or one per major customer segment).
- **Using the BMC for products that have not validated their value proposition.** The BMC assumes problem-solution fit and asks operational questions. For unvalidated early-stage ventures, the Lean Canvas is the appropriate starting point; switching to the BMC is itself a milestone that signals the company has moved from discovery into scaling.
- **Confusing "Customer Relationships" with "Channels."** Channels are the *delivery medium* (app store, sales call, email, retail shelf); Customer Relationships are the *type of bond* (self-service, dedicated account management, community, co-creation). The two interact—different relationship types are typically delivered through different channels—but they are distinct dimensions and conflating them collapses useful analysis.
- **Missing the link between blocks.** Channels that do not reach the listed Customer Segments. Key Activities that do not produce any listed Value Proposition. Key Resources that no Key Activity actually requires. The strength of the BMC comes from coherence across blocks; gaps and inconsistencies are themselves diagnostic findings worth surfacing.
- **Treating Cost Structure as a budget rather than a business-model element.** The Cost Structure block is meant to expose the *shape* of costs (cost-driven vs. value-driven, high fixed vs. high variable) in a way that informs strategy, not to substitute for a P&L. A line-by-line budget belongs in the financial model; the BMC needs the largest line items and their structural character.
- **Filling the canvas without cross-functional input.** The right side and the left side of the canvas tend to attract different functions; a canvas filled by one function alone is partial. Cross-functional review is where the inconsistencies surface and the canvas earns its value.
